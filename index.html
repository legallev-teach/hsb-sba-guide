Example: "According to the Trinidad and Tobago Ministry of Health (year), [relevant stat]. This is significant because…"
IMPORTANT
Do not just change the name on the group document and submit it. Your charts, your analysis, your reflection — these must be original. And your Introduction must be your own version, not a word-for-word copy. Two marks are not worth the risk.
Writing style: third person passive

Academic writing does not use "I". Use the third person passive. Here is the difference:

DON'T"I distributed the questionnaires through WhatsApp."
DO"Questionnaires were distributed through WhatsApp class groups."
DON'T"I found that 65% of students sleep less than 6 hours."
DO"It was found that 65% of students reported sleeping fewer than 6 hours per night."
DON'T"We chose this sample because it was convenient."
DO"This sample was selected due to its accessibility within the school."
Notice the pattern: the action word gets "was" or "were" before it. "Distributed" → "were distributed." "Found" → "was found." "Collected" → "was collected." Try it on any sentence from the group document.

STEP 5 OF 6
Write Your Analysis
What is analysis?
Analysis means looking at each chart and answering two questions: What does it show? (give a number) and Why does that happen? (explain the biology). That combination — number + explanation — is analysis.
ANALYSIS
What the data shows. Always use a number.
"65% said X."
DISCUSSION
Why it matters and what it means.
"This suggests that…"
In CSEC HSB, both go in the same section. The 4-step formula below covers both automatically.
The 4 Steps — what they are and what colour they are in the example
Step 1 — State the number. Name the figure and give the key percentage or count.
Step 2 — Compare. Is this number higher or lower for a different group? Compare two groups.
Step 3 — Explain using biology. Use specific terms: hormones, organs, body systems, named processes. This is the most important step.
Step 4 — Connect to the aim. Does this finding support what the study set out to find? Say so.
Red dashed = missing or weak. In the example tab, these show where a step was skipped.
Use all 4 steps for each chart
STEP 1 — State the number BLUE in example
Name the figure and give the main number
Start by naming the chart (Figure 1, Figure 2…). Give the key percentage or count.
"According to Figure 1, 72% of respondents reported playing football, making it the most popular sport among the group surveyed."
STEP 2 — Compare GREEN in example
Compare within the data
Is this number higher or lower than another group? Male vs female? Frequent vs infrequent?
"Of students who played sports 3–4 times per week, 94% agreed that sports improved their academic performance, compared to 86% of those who played less frequently."
STEP 3 — Explain the biology YELLOW in example
Use specific biology — this is the most important step
Do not say "because of lifestyle." Name hormones, organs, body systems, named processes.
"This may be explained by the fact that physical activity increases blood flow to the brain and stimulates the release of endorphins, which reduce cortisol levels that would otherwise interfere with concentration and memory."
STEP 4 — Connect to the aim PINK in example
Link back to the research objective
Does this finding support or challenge what was originally set out to find? What does it mean for the topic?
"This finding supports the aim of this study, which sought to determine whether sports participation has positive effects on the physical and mental health of secondary school students."
IMPORTANT — covers ALL your data
Your analysis is not just the 2–4 charts in Presentation of Data
This is the most common mistake. The Presentation of Data section shows 3–4 charts. But your Analysis must discuss all your findings — including charts that are only in the Appendix. Write at least one paragraph per major theme your data covered.
HOW TO THINK ABOUT IT
You collected data on many questions. The reader should understand what all of it found. Write at least one paragraph per major theme — awareness, attitude, behaviour, influencing factors, etc. Use your Appendix charts as evidence throughout.
Sentence starters
"According to Figure __, __ % of respondents…" "It was found that the majority of respondents…" "This was higher / lower among [group] compared to [group]…" "This may be attributed to the fact that…" "This finding suggests that…" "These results are consistent with the research objective because…" "The data indicates that respondents who… were more likely to…" "This is significant because it demonstrates that…"
Description is not analysis
Description: "The pie chart shows that 91% of respondents agreed that sports improves their mental health." — You just read the chart out loud.

Analysis: "The data indicates that 91% of respondents agreed that sports participation improves mental health. This may be due to the release of endorphins during physical activity, which reduce the perception of stress and promote positive mood. This finding strongly supports the aim of this study."

See the difference? Description stops after the number. Analysis keeps going.

ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION — ANONYMISED STUDENT WORK · Topic: Sports and wellbeing
Analysis and Discussion
PARAGRAPH 1 · Sample and sports played
PARAGRAPH 1 Respondents ranged in age from 13 to 20, the majority (almost 70%) aged 16–19. The respondents were approximately 60% male and 40% female. According to Figure 1, the top four sports played were Football (72%), Cricket (31%), Track and Field (21%) and Basketball (15%), indicating that the majority of students surveyed are actively engaged in sport. This suggests that sports participation is an integral part of the lifestyle of students at this school, making this sample well-suited to investigate the relationship between sports and wellbeing.
✦ STEP 1 — NUMBER STATED
Good. "Almost 70%" is specific and tells the reader exactly who was surveyed. Always give a percentage — never say "most" or "many" without a number to support it.
✦ STEP 1 — SAMPLE DESCRIPTION
Good. Describing the gender split helps the reader understand who the data represents. This kind of context belongs at the start of the analysis.
✦ STEP 1 — NUMBER STATED (Figure referenced)
Excellent — four sports named with exact percentages, figure referenced correctly. Notice how easy it is to follow because the numbers appear immediately after each sport name.
✦ COULD BE STRONGER — STEP 3 MISSING
This sentence is pure description. A biology explanation should follow here. For example: "Football's dominance may reflect its low equipment cost and team-based structure, which promotes social bonding — a key factor in mental health and community wellbeing."
✦ STEP 4 — CONNECTED TO AIM
Good attempt. The student ties the finding back to why the sample is relevant. This paragraph would be stronger if a Step 3 sentence appeared before this one.
⚠ WHAT THIS PARAGRAPH NEEDS
It describes the sample well (Step 1) but has no biology explanation (Step 3). After naming the sports, add one sentence explaining why football dominates — low cost, team structure, cultural presence in T&T — using a biological or social lens. Then the Step 4 closing sentence lands with more weight.
PARAGRAPH 2 · Mental health and academic performance
PARAGRAPH 2 According to Figure 2, almost all respondents (91%) agreed that sports improves their mental wellbeing, while 89% reported a positive effect on academic performance, 79% on teamwork, and 89% on physical health. Of students who played sports three to four times per week, 94.1% believed sport helped their school performance. Even students who played less frequently — once to three times per month — showed an 85.7% agreement rate. This may be explained by the fact that physical activity increases blood flow to the brain and stimulates the release of endorphins, which reduce cortisol levels that would otherwise interfere with concentration and memory formation. Both groups agreed that sports is beneficial, but the data suggests that higher frequency of participation leads to greater perceived academic benefit.
✦ STEP 1 — NUMBERS STATED
Very strong. Four findings given with exact percentages in a single sentence. The student names what each percentage refers to — mental wellbeing, academics, teamwork, health. This is exactly the level of precision needed.
✦ STEP 2 — COMPARISON (EXCELLENT)
One of the strongest sentences in the analysis. Two groups compared — frequent players vs. infrequent players — with specific percentages for both. The reference to an edge case is smart. This is exactly what CXC examiners look for.
✦ STEP 3 — BIOLOGY USED
Good biology. "Blood flow to the brain," "endorphins," and "cortisol levels" are all named correctly. To make it even stronger, specify what endorphins do: they bind to opioid receptors and produce feelings of euphoria, reducing anxiety and improving focus.
✦ COULD BE STRONGER — STEP 4 MISSING
This closing sentence is still in Step 2 territory. The paragraph needs a Step 4 sentence connecting the finding to the aim. For example: "These findings are consistent with the aim of this investigation, which sought to determine whether sports participation positively affects the health and academic performance of secondary school students. The data strongly suggests that it does — and that the effect increases with frequency."
⚠ WHAT THIS PARAGRAPH NEEDS
Steps 1, 2 and 3 are all present and done well. The only missing piece is a proper Step 4 closing sentence connecting the findings back to the aim. Add it and this paragraph is complete.
PARAGRAPH 3 · Lifestyle changes and parental influence
PARAGRAPH 3 Figure 3 reveals that almost 60% of students have made personal health changes — such as adjustments to diet and sleep habits — in order to improve their sports performance. This shows that participation in sports motivates students to adopt healthier lifestyle behaviours and is therefore an effective strategy for addressing the risk of lifestyle diseases among young people. Furthermore, students whose parents actively encouraged sports participation were significantly more likely to play frequently: 69.2% of those who were encouraged played every day or three to four times per week. In contrast, the only student whose parents discouraged sport played the least frequently of all respondents. This highlights the important role that parental support plays in shaping adolescent health behaviours.
✦ STEP 1 — NUMBER STATED (Figure referenced)
Good. The student named specific health changes (diet and sleep) rather than just saying "health changes." Specificity is what separates description from analysis. The figure reference is correct.
✦ COULD BE STRONGER — BIG CLAIM NEEDS STEP 3
This sentence makes a significant claim — that sports addresses lifestyle disease risk — but provides no biological evidence. It needs a Step 3 sentence: "Diseases such as type 2 diabetes and hypertension — both highly prevalent in Trinidad and Tobago — are directly linked to physical inactivity and poor diet. Regular sports participation reduces adipose tissue, improves insulin sensitivity, and lowers resting blood pressure."
✦ STEP 2 — COMPARISON (EXCELLENT)
One of the best sentences in the whole analysis. Two groups compared — encouraging parents vs. discouraging parents — with a specific percentage. The reference to the outlier ("the only student whose parents discouraged sport played the least") is smart analytical thinking.
✦ COULD BE STRONGER — STEPS 3 AND 4 MISSING
The paragraph ends on a Step 2 observation. It needs both Step 3 biology and Step 4 connection to the aim. "Research in developmental psychology indicates that parental encouragement fosters intrinsic motivation — the internal drive to maintain healthy behaviours. This finding is relevant to the aim, as it suggests that interventions targeting parental attitudes may be as important as those targeting students directly."
⚠ WHAT THIS PARAGRAPH NEEDS
Two problems. First, the lifestyle disease claim needs biological backing — name the diseases (diabetes, hypertension) and the biological mechanism (insulin sensitivity, blood pressure). Second, the paragraph closes on a comparison (Step 2) with no biology (Step 3) and no connection to the aim (Step 4). Always end each paragraph with Steps 3 and 4 before moving on.

POLISHED VERSION — ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION
Analysis and Discussion
PARAGRAPH 1 · Sample characteristics and sports played
According to Figure 1, respondents ranged in age from 13 to 20, with the majority (almost 70%) falling between the ages of 16 and 19. The sample was approximately 60% male and 40% female. The most popular sports were Football (72%), Cricket (31%), Track and Field (21%), and Basketball (15%). Football's dominance may reflect its accessibility — it requires minimal equipment and is deeply embedded in Caribbean school culture — as well as its team-based structure, which research links to the development of social bonds and a sense of belonging, both of which are established protective factors for mental health. Students who played sports more frequently (three to four times per week) were significantly more likely to report high levels of physical confidence than those who played once a month or less (88% compared to 61%). It was found that students who engaged in sport regularly were more likely to exhibit proactive health behaviours, which is directly relevant to the aim of this investigation — to determine whether sports participation positively affects the health and wellbeing of secondary school students at this school.
PARAGRAPH 2 · Mental health and academic performance
According to Figure 2, almost all respondents (91%) agreed that sports participation improves their mental wellbeing, while 89% reported a positive effect on academic performance, 79% on teamwork ability, and 89% on physical health. Of students who played sports three to four times per week, 94.1% believed sport helped their school performance — compared to 85.7% among those who played only once to three times per month. This pattern may be explained by the physiological effects of regular physical activity: exercise stimulates the release of endorphins, which bind to opioid receptors in the brain and reduce the perception of stress, while simultaneously lowering circulating cortisol — a hormone that, at elevated levels, impairs the hippocampus and reduces the capacity for memory formation and sustained concentration. The increase in cerebral blood flow that accompanies aerobic activity further supports cognitive function. These findings are consistent with the research objective, which sought to determine whether regular sports participation has measurable benefits for the academic and mental health outcomes of young people. The data strongly suggests that it does, and that the benefit increases with frequency of participation.
PARAGRAPH 3 · Lifestyle behaviours and parental influence
According to Figure 3, almost 60% of students reported making deliberate personal health changes — including modifications to diet, sleep patterns, and hydration habits — in order to improve their sports performance. This finding is significant because non-communicable diseases such as type 2 diabetes mellitus and hypertension are among the leading causes of premature death in Trinidad and Tobago, and both are directly linked to physical inactivity, excess adipose tissue, and poor dietary habits. Regular sports participation addresses these risk factors through multiple biological mechanisms: it reduces body fat percentage, improves insulin sensitivity by increasing glucose uptake in skeletal muscle, and lowers resting blood pressure through adaptations in cardiac output and vascular tone. Furthermore, it was found that students whose parents actively encouraged sports participation were significantly more likely to play frequently — 69.2% of those who received parental encouragement played every day or three to four times per week, compared to the one student whose parents discouraged participation, who played the least of all respondents. Research in developmental psychology indicates that parental encouragement fosters intrinsic motivation — the internal drive to sustain healthy behaviours — which is particularly influential during adolescence. These findings suggest that interventions aimed at improving youth health outcomes in this community may need to target parental attitudes alongside student behaviour, and are directly relevant to the aim of this investigation.
